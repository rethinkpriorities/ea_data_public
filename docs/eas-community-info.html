Association with other satisfaction measures and self-reported reasons

We consider âhow do stated satisfaction measures (including open-responses) in each area relate to the overall satisfaction measure?â

Some caveats:

  • This may not tell us what causes overall satisfaction with EA, nor even the extent to which the stated levels of satisfaction are important intermediate drivers of satisfaction. Individuals who are less satisfied in certain areas may differ systematically from others in both observable and unobservable ways. E.g., people with a highly-positive demeanor might be particularly likely (A) to report they âagree: I feel that I am a part of the effective altruism communityâ and might also (B) report high levels of overall satisfaction with EA. But (A) need not be the cause of (B); (A) and (B) may simply be picking up particular personality trait that we cannot control for with other response data.

  • Even in considering the question âwhy did you give the two ratings above?â we cannot be certain that people know the deep causal reasons why they chose one rating versus another. We donât always know our own motives; there is some evidence suggesting that people attribute explanations to even random events and justify choices that were actually made out of their control.*

::: {.marginnote} See, e.g., self-serving attribution bias (Wikipedia link) and Coherent Arbitrariness (arielyCoherentArbitrarinessStable2003?) :::

Thus, we do not construct an elaborate model of this. However, we still report on these associations, for descriptive purposes. (Thoughts in fold below.)

This still seems likely to be somewhat informative. Consider, hypothetically:

  • Case I. Suppose (hypothetically) that those who complained about, e.g., lack of social interaction social skills tended to express great dissatisfaction with EA versus

  • Case IIâ¦ that those who complained lack of social interaction/social skills tended to nonetheless be satisfied overall with EA If we observed Case I (relative to Case II) we would be at least somewhat more convinced that âlack of social interactionâ was an important cause of dissatisfaction for some individuals.

Predictors of satisfaction: descriptive and causally-suggestive

Ordered logit models

Here we are not interested in a simple predictive model. We are not trying to âpredict satisfactionâ for any selection or planning purpose. Thus âfitting the dataâ (or even âfitting set-aside dataâ) is not the goal.

We want to answer specific questions like âdo women tend to be less satisfied, all else equal,â with implications for:

  1. Where are the dissatisfied people? â¦ These may be the groups we would get the greatest payoff from reaching out to.

  2. What sorts of people become dissatisfied, and along which paths? This may offer clues as to what we can do better. Here we may even want to introduce things that are not strictly ex-ante (like actions and EA activities they are involved in). However, we need to be careful the problem of âcollidersâ in drawing our inferencesâ e.g.Â we would probably expect âless satisfied peopleâ to be âless likely to undertake substantial activities.â

The âall else equalâ is also challenging. E.g., in considering whether âwomen are less satisfied,â do we want to âcontrol forâ income, occupation, or even cause priorities? If we donât control for those, we might be getting at something that is only indirectly related to gender and misses the point. But if we do control for them, we may not be diagnosing the right thing.*

*Perhaps analogously, in considering wage differentials between men and women, one might consider controlling for physical strength, or perhaps for differences in verbal ability. But the former tends to be systematically lower in women (for biological reasons), and the latter seems to be systematically higher for females. If we âcontrolâ for each of these, we may not get a good descriptive picture of the wage differences between similar men and women, as we are eliminating a channel that may be an inherent part of the the âgenderâ characteristic we are interested in.

Below, we construct an ordered logit model, reporting the odds ratios â these can be loosely interpreted as the ârelative probability of stating a 1 unit higher level of satisfaction,â relative to the base group. E.g., if the coefficient for males is 1.5, this means that, controlling for everything else in the model, comparing any two levels of satisfaction (2 versus 1, 9 versus 8, etc) males are approximately 50% more likely to be in the higher group.**

** Note that the ordered logit model imposes a particular functional form: a variable must have proportional relative âeffectâ on the probability of being in a higher versus a lower category, with this proportional âeffectâ being the same between any comparison of adjacent categories.

The first (âStrict Ex-anteâ) columns consider only those âex-anteâ characteristics that (at least if accurately reported), could not themselves have been influenced by the individualâs experience in EA. Still, we are hesitant to give a direct causal interpretation here.*

* âBeing Jazzy caused people to be more satisfied with EAâ is a strong statement. Differential selection and attrition could be driving this. E.g., less satisfied Jazzers may have been relatively less likely to join EA or complete the survey, or dropped out relatively more. The relationship may also be driven by unobserved mediators. E..g, Jazzy people tend to be happier people and thus report higher satisfaction.

The next columns (âMaybe exogenousâ) add two characteristics, which, while not entirely ex-ante, are (arguably) unlikely to be substantially impacted by the EA experience, and thus perhaps not vulnerable to reverse-causality issues.** As a robustness check, these columns also include (hidden) controls for quartiles (25% percentile groupings) of age and time-in EA. The results here are nearly identical to the first columns.

** In other models, not shown, we also considered income (without imputing values where this was missing); this reduced our sample size considerably but seemed to have a near-zero relationship with satisfaction.

Holding all else (in the above models) equalâ¦

  • Males were substantially more satisfied, with a roughly 50% higher chance of reporting a higher category of satisfaction.

  • Those living in the USA were a bit less satisfied.

  • Older participants were somewhat less satisfied (with the probability of being in a higher category declining by about 2% per year-of-age).

  • Years involved in EA also relates to lower satisfaction.***

    *** We can separately identify âageâ coefficients and âtenure in EAâ coefficients here as people joined EA at different ages. However, the interpretation is challenging, particularly if we allow for both age, period, cohort, and tenure effects. Note that these are not standardized coefficients so the âsizeâ of the effects are not comparable to the binary coefficients; we report a standardized version in graphs below.

Note that these relationships also generally persist in the models below, which also include prioritization and âfirst-heardâ measures.

The table above considers the relationship of satisfaction to cause-prioritization, with three distinct approaches.

  1. âTop-priorityâ:

This model focuses on whether the respondent assigned one of the âlong termâ (or low-probability high-risk) causes an importance as high or higher than any of the other non-long-term causes. The long-term causes were defined as Broad longtermism, Existential risks, AI risks, Biosecurity/pandemic preparedness, and Nuclear security. The single âAnimal welfareâ¦â category is treated similarly.

  1. âPriority ratingsâ

Here we focus on the simple relationship between the âprioritization scoreâ assigned a numerical value ranging from 1 (for âI do not think any resources should be devoted to this causeâ) to 5 (for "I think this cause should be the top priority). We construct several indices for each person averaging this score acrossâ¦

  • Long-termist categories (mentioned above)
  • Meta causes: Cause prioritization, Meta, and EA movement building

  1. âPriorities (relative)â

    This model includes a term for âAll causes: Mean prioritizationâ for the individual EA respondent, as well as terms for how his/her mean response to the other cause groups compared to this mean. Arguably, this controls for the tendency of some people to interpret the cause priority questions differently overall, while allowing us to detect the relationship to individualâs relative prioritization.

Summarizing the results of these above models:

  • Those assigning long-term causes as among the top priority (âLong term= a top priorityâ), or assigning it a high priority in general (âLong-term-causes: meanâ¦â), were more satisfied

  • Those assigning high priority to causes overall tended to be more satisfied (âall causes: mean prioritizationâ)

  • Relative to the individualâs mean prioritization, prioitizing

    • meta-causes, long-term causes, and animal welfare was associated with higher satisfaction,
    • however, prioritizing immediate causes relatively highly was (weakly) associated with lower satisfaction.

As above, these models suggest that âmales are more satisfied,â âolder people are less satisfied,â and âUS-residents are less satisfied,â holding other things equal.

Below, we also consider whether satisfaction differs among those âfirst hearing of EAâ from EA from distinct sources. This might indicate differences in the extent to which each source, as a âpath towards EAâ is doing driving satisfaction. However, the potential for differential selection makes us reluctant to infer this to be a causal relationship.
